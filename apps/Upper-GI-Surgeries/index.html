üîó Gastric Band

The Gastric Band surgery is typically performed on patients who have spent years steadily consuming high-calorie, indulgent foods such as whipped cream, congealed foods, and chocolates. This diet, combined with prolonged periods in a horizontal position, has necessitated the surgical procedure to help control overeating.

  • The application of an inflatable silicone band around the upper part of the stomach creates a restrictive pouch.
  • This limits the amount of food (e.g., french fries and ice cream) that can be eaten at one time.
  • The goal is to inspire the patient to ‚Äústop eating‚Äù when they feel full.
  • Common in certain populations (e.g., southern West Virginia).

üìå Complications of Gastric Band

  • Stomal Stenosis: Most common complication due to the band being too tight. Classic history includes vomiting. Barium exam shows a dilated proximal pouch with leftover food (e.g., Wendy‚Äôs cheeseburger) floating in the barium.
  • Gastric Band Erosion and Gastric Leak: Can occur from pressure-related ischemia. Classic look on CT is the band within the lumen of the stomach.
  • Band Slippage: Most likely to be tested. The band should be positioned around the 2 o‚Äôclock position with a phi angle between 45-50 degrees. If the angle is off (more flat), it indicates the band has slipped, potentially leading to obstruction.

üîÑ Billroth I

Billroth I is a type of upper GI surgery where a partial gastrectomy is performed, and the remaining portion of the stomach is directly attached to the duodenum.

  • Performed for Gastric Cancer, Pyloric Dysfunction, or Ulcers in the distal stomach.
  • Risks include dumping syndrome and afferent loop syndrome.
  • Less post-operative gastritis compared to Billroth II.

üîÑ Billroth II

Billroth II is a type of upper GI surgery where a partial gastrectomy is performed, and the remaining portion of the stomach is attached to the jejunum. Unlike Billroth I, the pylorus is removed and the proximal stomach is sewn directly to the jejunum.

  • Performed for Gastric Cancer, Ulcers in the distal stomach, or Pyloric Dysfunction.
  • Risks include dumping syndrome, afferent loop syndrome, and more early post-operative complications compared to Billroth I.
  • Associated with a higher risk of developing adenocarcinoma in the gastric remnant 10-20 years post-surgery.

üîÑ Roux-en-Y

Roux-en-Y is a type of upper GI surgery where the stomach is divided to create a pouch, which is then attached to the jejunum. The excluded stomach attaches to the duodenum as per normal. The jejunum is connected to another jejunum segment to form the bottom of the "Y."

  • Performed for obesity, gastric cancer, or as an alternative to Billroth surgeries if the primary lesion has invaded the duodenum or pancreas.
  • Pros include less reflux and lower risk of recurrent gastric cancer.
  • Risks include leaks, gallstones, fistulas, internal hernias, and other complications.

üìå Anastomosis Points in Roux-en-Y

  • Gastro-Jejunal (efferent) Limb: The "feeding limb" attached to the gastric pouch.
  • Bilio-Pancreatic (afferent) Limb: The limb attached to the excluded stomach and duodenum.
  • Jejuno-Jejunal Anastomosis: Connects the afferent limb to another jejunal segment to form the "Y."

üìå Dumping Syndrome

Dumping Syndrome is a group of symptoms including diarrhea, nausea, and feeling lightheaded or tired after a meal. It is caused by rapid gastric emptying and is commonly seen with Billroth II and early post-operative periods after Roux-en-Y.

  • Related to rapid transit of undigested food from the stomach.
  • Diagnosed with a gastric emptying study.
  • Treatment typically involves conversion of Billroth to Roux-en-Y and avoiding high-carbohydrate foods.
  • Trivia: Insert a joke about Taco Bell or a family member's cooking to remember the syndrome.

üìå Afferent Loop Syndrome

Afferent Loop Syndrome is a potential complication post Billroth II and Roux-en-Y surgeries. It occurs when an obstruction (extrinsic or intrinsic) blocks the upstream afferent limb, causing secretions, bile, and pancreatic juice to build up.

  • Presentation includes belly pain often with bilious vomiting.
  • Imaging shows a fluid-filled ‚ÄúU-shaped‚Äù loop of bowel adjacent to the pancreas.
  • Can lead to gallbladder dilation and pancreatitis.

üìå Leak

Leaks typically occur early (within 10 days post-op) at the anastomosis of the stomach and small bowel (gastrojejunostomy).

  • Best detected with a water-soluble oral contrast exam in Fluoroscopy 1-2 days post-op.
  • Positive leak is indicated by contrast outside the lumen of the stomach or bowel.
  • Risk of barium peritonitis if barium leaks.
  • Trivia: A heart rate > 120 is a reliable sign of perforation.

üìå Gastro-Gastric Fistula

Seen in Roux-en-Y patients with weight gain years after surgery, allowing access to the excluded stomach.

  • The fistula allows ingestion of more food without discomfort.
  • Often a chronic process with little pain.

üìå Marginal Ulcer

Ulcers at or near the gastrojejunostomy anastomosis are referred to as marginal ulcers.

  • Most common location is just distal to the gastrojejunostomy anastomosis.
  • Gamesmanship: Marginal ulcers are classically solitary. Multiple large ulcers in this region may suggest chronic jejunal ischemia.

üìå Jejunogastric Intussusception

A rare complication where the jejunum herniates back into the stomach, usually the efferent limb, causing gastric obstruction.

  • High mortality rate in the acute form.
  • Requires immediate surgical intervention.

üöß Small Bowel Obstruction (SBO)

üîç Types of SBO

Understanding the types of Small Bowel Obstruction is crucial, especially in patients with a history of Roux-en-Y surgery. SBO can be categorized into two main types: Simple Mechanical and Closed Loop Obstruction.

  • Simple Mechanical Obstruction: A bowel loop is obstructed at a single point.
  • Closed Loop Obstruction: A bowel loop is obstructed at two or more adjacent points, forming a closed loop.

üìå Simple Mechanical Obstruction

In simple mechanical obstruction, there is a single lead point causing obstruction, leading to intestinal distention.

  • Accumulation of gas and fluids upstream from the obstruction.
  • Imaging shows air-fluid levels.
  • Treatment centers around decompression using NG tubes.

üìå Closed Loop Obstruction

Closed Loop Obstruction involves two or more transition points, creating a loop that can quickly lead to ischemia and requires surgical intervention.

  • Presentation includes more severe and rapid onset of ischemic symptoms.
  • Imaging may show a "beak sign" and a "radial layout" of the bowel loops.
  • High risk for ischemia and perforation.

üìå Beak Sign and Radial Layout

  • Beak Sign: Three ‚Äúbeaks‚Äù or transition points indicating obstruction.
  • Radial Layout: Vessels and loops converge towards a central point, often seen in twisted bowel segments.

üåÄ Internal Hernias

Internal hernias are a potential complication post Roux-en-Y surgery, especially in the retrocolic subtype where a defect is created in the transverse mesocolon.

  • Types:
    • Ante-Colic: Roux limb passes in front of the transverse colon.
    • Retro-Colic: Roux limb passes behind the transverse colon, requiring a defect in the transverse mesocolon.
  • Potential sites for internal hernias:
    • Defect in the Transverse Mesocolon
    • Mesenteric Defect at the Enteroenterostomy
    • Petersen's Defect (behind the Roux limb mesentery)
  • Risk Factors:
    • Ante-Colic or Retro-Colic configuration
    • Surgical defects created during Roux-en-Y
    • Adhesions from previous surgeries
    • Increased mobility due to laparoscopic procedures
    • Degree of weight loss (less mesenteric fat allows easier twisting)

üìå Petersen's Defect

  • Located behind the Roux limb mesentery in retrocolic Roux-en-Y.
  • Potential site for internal hernias.
  • Can lead to Closed Loop Obstruction.
