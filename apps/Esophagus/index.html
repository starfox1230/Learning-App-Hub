üîç Diverticula

Zenker Diverticulum

Pulsion diverticulum located in the hypopharynx (back). Site of weakness is the Killian Dehiscence or triangle.

Killian-Jamieson Diverticulum

Pulsion diverticulum located in the cervical esophagus. It protrudes through an area of weakness below the attachment of the cricopharyngeus muscle and lateral to the ligaments that help suspend the esophagus on the cricoid cartilage.

Epiphrenic Diverticula

Located just above the diaphragm, usually on the right. These are para-esophageal hernias.

Traction Diverticulum

Mid esophageal, often triangular in shape, resulting from scarring (e.g., granulomatous disease or TB). They do not empty and contain muscle in their walls.

Esophageal Pseudodiverticulosis

Multiple small outpouchings due to dilated submucosal glands, often associated with chronic reflux esophagitis.

üî• Reflux Esophagitis

Fold Thickening

A common cause of fold thickening, which if left unchecked, can lead to serious complications such as strictures and Barrett's esophagus.

Spectrum of Aggression

Unchecked reflux can progress from mild fold thickening to severe fold thickening, leading to strictures and Barrett's esophagus, and eventually cancer.

Barrett's Esophagus

A precursor to esophageal adenocarcinoma, characterized by high stricture with an associated hiatal hernia.

Types of Cancer

Squamous Cell Carcinoma

Adenocarcinoma - develops secondary to chronic reflux. On barium, look for "irregular contour" and "abrupt (shouldered) edges."

Adenocarcinoma

Associated with chronic reflux and Barrett's Esophagus. Typically located in the lower esophagus.

Critical Stages

T3 vs T4: Early stages are distinguished by endoscopy, while advanced stages (T3 and T4) require CT scans to assess invasion into adjacent structures.

Treatment Options

Fundoplication after failed H2 blockers and PPIs, esophagectomy, and in severe cases, palliative care options.

Fundoplication

The gastric fundus is wrapped around the lower end of the esophagus and stitched in place, reinforcing the lower esophageal sphincter. Types include:

  • Nissen: 360-degree wrap.
  • Toupet: Loose wraps (<360 degrees).

Early Complications

Esophageal obstruction or narrowing from post-op edema or overly tight wraps, peaking around week 2. Barium studies may show total or near-total obstruction.

Failure of Fundoplication

Recurrent reflux due to telescoping of the GE junction through the wrap (slipped Nissen), often caused by a short esophagus.

Short Esophagus Treatment

Collis Gastroplasty: Lengthening of the esophagus followed by fundoplication.

üß† Trivia

Hand Placement for Barium Swallow

When performing a thoracic barium swallow exam, the barium cup should be placed in the Left Posterior Oblique (LPO) position to avoid obscuring the field of view.

Esophageal Spasm

Characterized by "corkscrew" tertiary contractions favoring the distal esophagus. Nutcracker esophagus requires manometric findings (>180mmHg).

Esophageal Pseudodiverticulosis

Multiple small outpouchings resembling dilated excretory mucosal ducts, often associated with esophageal strictures (90%), reflux, and candidiasis.

Plummer-Vinson Syndrome

Characterized by iron deficiency anemia, dysphagia, and esophageal webs.

Normal Submucosal Venous Plexus

Higher up and shallow in the esophagus.
