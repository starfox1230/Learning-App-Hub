Tc-MDP: The workhorse of skeletal imaging is methylene diphosphonate (MDP) - which is tagged with Tc-99m.

Mechanism

MDP is a phosphate analog that binds with the crystalline hydroxyapatite in bone via a process called chemisorption. Chemisorption can also be described as ‚Äúphysicochemical adsorption‚Äù on multiple choice exams - for the purpose of confusing you. Distribution is based on osteoblastic activity and to a lesser extent blood flow.

  • Osteoblasts: Active in bone formation.
  • Osteoclasts: Active in bone resorption.

In the growing skeleton (peds), tracer/MDP deposition is also seen at epiphyseal growth plates and osteochondral junctions. Hot growth plates are normal on MDP bone scan.

Gamesmanship: MDP and HDP are both bone agents so don‚Äôt get confused if they say HDP to purposefully confuse you.

üî¨ Tc-MDP Bone Scans

MDP Uptake Depends On:

  • Osteoblastic activity (why pure lytic lesions can be cold)
  • Blood Flow

Normal MDP Uptake / Localization Occurs At:

  • Bone
  • Epiphyses in kids
  • Kidney (not seen or very faint)
  • Bladder
  • Breasts (especially in young women)
  • Soft tissues - low levels

After preparation of the tracer (15-25mCi) it is injected into the patient. Then you wait 2-4 hours to let the tracer clear from the soft tissues (so you can see the bones). Renal function is critical for this soft tissue clearing.

‚ö†Ô∏è Abnormal Distribution

Increased focal uptake is very nonspecific and can indicate bone turnover due to various conditions such as metastatic deposits, arthritis, or healing fractures.

Sneaky Situations:

  • Skull Sutures: Normal persistence, but marked suture uptake may suggest renal osteodystrophy.
  • Breast Uptake: Mild diffuse uptake is normal, especially in younger women. Focal uptake can indicate cancer.
  • Renal Cortex Activity: Should have normal uptake. Hotter renal cortex than adjacent lumbar spine suggests hemochromatosis.
  • Liver Uptake: Can indicate contamination, primary hepatoma, metastases, amyloidosis, or liver necrosis.
  • Spleen Uptake: Commonly shows auto-infarcted spleen in sickle cell patients.

üí´ Super Scans

A "Super Scan" is characterized by diffuse skeletal metastatic activity with no visible kidneys, indicating that the tracer is predominantly taken up by the bones.

Types of Super Scans:

  • Diffuse Mets: Diffuse skeletal metastatic activity (commonly from breast and prostate cancers).
  • Metabolic: From metabolic bone pathologies such as hyperparathyroidism, renal osteodystrophy, Paget's disease, or severe thyrotoxicosis.

üéØ Specific Cancers

Different cancers have distinct patterns of bone metastases. Understanding these patterns can aid in diagnosis and management.

Common Patterns:

  • Prostate Cancer: Loves bone mets, especially in the spine.
  • Lung Cancer: Tends to metastasize to the appendicular skeleton.
  • Breast Cancer: Most common metastases to the spine.
  • Neuroblastoma: Frequently metastasizes to the metaphysis of long bones.

ü¶¥ Bone Scan vs Skeletal Survey

Bone Scan:

  • More sensitive for blastic metastases.
  • Utilizes Tc-MDP or other tracers.
  • Preferred for detecting bone metastases from various cancers.

Skeletal Survey:

  • More sensitive for lytic metastases.
  • Uses plain radiographs (X-rays).
  • Preferred for evaluating osseous involvement in multiple myeloma.

üîÑ Post Treatment

Flare Phenomenon:

The ‚Äúflare‚Äù typically occurs around 2 weeks to 3 months post-treatment and manifests as an increase in both the number and size of lesions, potentially mimicking disease progression.

  • On plain film, lesions should become more sclerotic.
  • After 3 months, lesions should improve, confirming a response to therapy.

Radiation Therapy Changes:

  • Acute Phase: Warm uptake within the radiation port peaking around 2-3 months.
  • Late Phase: Cold uptake at 6 months indicates permanent change; hot uptake later suggests recurrent disease.

üõ†Ô∏è Prosthesis Evaluation

Differentiating infection from aseptic loosening in prostheses is challenging. Bone scan findings can be nonspecific, requiring combined imaging techniques for accurate diagnosis.

Key Points:

  • Bone scan shows diffusely increased activity with Tc-MDP in infection.
  • Aseptic loosening shows focal uptake along the stem and lesser trochanter.
  • Combined Tc-Sulfur Colloid and WBC imaging differentiates infection from loosening.
  • A negative bone scan excludes loosening or infection.

ü¶∂ Neuropathic Foot

Neuropathic foot is commonly seen in diabetics and can present challenges in distinguishing between arthritis changes and infection using Tc-MDP bone scans.

Key Points:

  • Most commonly seen in the tarsal and tarsal-metatarsal joints (60%).
  • Combined marrow and WBC studies help differentiate infection.
  • Fourth phase imaging at 24 hours may help distinguish bone from delayed soft tissue clearance.
  • Tc99 HMPAO WBC imaging is preferred in children and small body parts like hands and feet.
